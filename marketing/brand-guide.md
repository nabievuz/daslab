# DasLab Brand Guide

> Version 0.1 — drafted by Content Lead, pending CMO approval.

---

## Brand Voice

DasLab speaks like a **brilliant colleague who happens to be an expert** — never a sales pitch, never a textbook. We are precise, energizing, and human.

---

## Three Messaging Pillars

### 1. Clarity at Scale
Data is overwhelming. DasLab cuts through noise and delivers answers, not more dashboards.

- Lead with insight, not feature lists.
- Use concrete numbers and outcomes when possible ("3× faster", "half the queries").
- Avoid vague claims: no "best-in-class", "world-class", or "robust".

**Sample headline:** "Your data pipeline, demystified."

---

### 2. Trusted by Builders
DasLab is built by engineers who ran into these problems themselves. That credibility is our moat.

- Reference technical specifics — we are not afraid of stack names, schema complexity, or scale numbers.
- Acknowledge hard problems honestly; don't oversell ease.
- Use "we" sparingly and only when it reflects a real shared experience with the reader.

**Sample headline:** "Production-grade data infrastructure, without the ops burden."

---

### 3. Speed Without Sacrifice
Modern data teams need to move fast. DasLab removes the friction — without trading away reliability or transparency.

- Emphasize iteration speed, not just initial setup time.
- Pair every speed claim with a quality or safety signal.
- Avoid "magic" framing; explain why it's fast.

**Sample headline:** "Ship analytics 10× faster. Sleep soundly doing it."

## Tone-of-Voice Rules

| Situation | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| Product descriptions | Active voice, present tense | Passive constructions ("is powered by") |
| Blog / long-form | Conversational, first-person perspective OK | Jargon walls, excessive acronyms |
| Error messages / UI copy | Plain, direct, actionable | Apologetic filler ("We're sorry for the inconvenience") |
| Social / short-form | Punchy — lead with the payoff | Open with a question; avoid em-dashes overuse |
| Technical docs | Precise, imperative verbs ("Run", "Configure") | Hedging language ("you might want to consider") |
| Email subject lines | Specific and benefit-led | Clickbait; caps-lock urgency |

### Always
- Write for a senior data engineer who values their time.
- Edit to 80% of the first draft length.
- Use the Oxford comma.
- Prefer short sentences. If a sentence needs a semicolon, split it.

### Never
- Use exclamation points in product copy (one maximum per email).
- Call the user "users" in customer-facing text — say "your team" or "you".
- Describe features without linking to outcomes.
- Use "leverage" as a verb.
